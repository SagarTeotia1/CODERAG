Finally, in terms of sanitary controls, from 1 January 2005, exports of food from the Community have to comply with the rules established in Article 12 of the general Food Law Regulation (EC) No 178/2002 of the Parliament and of the Council of 28 January 2002 laying down the general principles and requirements of food law, establishing the European Food Safety Authority and laying down procedures in matters of food safety, OJ L 31 , 1.2.2002 . According to that Article, if third countries have set their own level of protection for a particular food, exporting countries must then comply with the requirements set by importing countries. Where it is not the case, the food intended for export must comply with the relevant requirements of Community food law. Finally, if there is no relevant Community food law requirement and the third country has not set any specific requirements applicable to imports, food can only be exported if the competent authorities of the country of destination have expressly agreed and if the food is not injurious to health or unsafe. ee Implementation Guidance Document available at the following address : http://europa.eu.int/comm/food/food/foodlaw/guidance/index_en.htm However, before 1 January this year, there were no Community legal provisions to arrange such trade as it was based on bilateral agreements between a Member State and a third country trading partner. Most third countries would of course accept only poultry meat exported from the EU with the same quality and food safety standards as the poultry meat that is sold in the EU itself, but due to the lack of competence in the matter at that time, the EU is not aware of any particular bilateral arrangements to the contrary. Once the product has left the Community, the sanitary conditions are no longer controlled by EU authorities. The transporters/importers are responsible for the maintenance of good sanitary conditions during shipment, in particular the continuation of the cold chain.
