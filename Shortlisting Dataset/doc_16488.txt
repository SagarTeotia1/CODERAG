The report calls upon the Council to examine the question of the language options of the website of its Presidencies, in order to ensure that the entire population of the EU has easy access to information. Context In 2004, the petitioner, an association for the defence of the German language, wrote to the Dutch and the Luxembourg governments to ask them to offer the internet presentations they were to provide when holding the Presidency not only in English and French but also in German. Both the Dutch and the Luxembourg governments rejected the complainant's request. On 22 January 2005, the complainant wrote to the Council and, in its reply of 4 March 2005, the Council pointed out that the Member State holding the Presidency was solely responsible for the information published on its website. The Ombudsman was not satisfied with the Council reply, and, therefore, made a proposal for a friendly solution inviting the Council to consider the complainant's request that the Presidencies' websites should be made available in German. The Ombudsman argues that these websites do not only complement the Council's website, but go far beyond the contents of the latter. Key points of the report The report endorses the conclusions of the Ombudsman, namely that the Council, like every EU institution, is, in the first instance, itself responsible for the websites of its presidency and the languages used thereon.
